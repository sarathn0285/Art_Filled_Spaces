Introduction & Randomness from “10 PRINT CHR$(205.5+RND(1)); : GOTO 10”, by Nick Montfort et al, 2013:

The introduction lays out how code and coding languages are cultural artifacts created with aesthetics and functionality. These choices can tell us a lot about the societies that create them. With computers we can assign meaning to almost anything. Someone decided that “RND” should mean random and not “RnD”, or even “$$$”. These choices can do things like limit accessibility. It’s much easier to understand random if the code refers to it as “RND” as opposed to “$$$”.

The book also explains how randomness has always been essential to forms of play and creativity. Although code and computer systems may seem deterministic, they are filled with cultural meaning and aesthetic choices that rely on balancing levels of randomness. The most relatable experience I have was that of watching my dad play online poker on our family computer growing up. In games like these, elements like difficulty can be controlled with randomness algorithms. If a computer controlled opponent is too random, they can be too hard to predict and feel non-human, but give them just the right amount of randomness and you now have an adequately challenging opponent. In AI, features like “temperature” control how random or reproducible the AI’s answer will be. If you want your AI to be more “creative”, you can up the randomness of its answers! Even non-digital artists use randomness to generate creativity, like when a painter throws paint at a canvas instead of meticulously painting each stroke. Randomness is and has always been an important piece of human creativity.

Chapter 3 from “Hamlet on the Holodeck” by Janet Murray, 1997:

Murray begins this chapter with tracing evolution of storytelling from the birth of cinema to contemporary digital narratives. She references the “Arrival of a Train at La Ciotat Station”, illustrating how early filmmakers transformed existing technology into a new artistic medium through innovative narrative techniques. It reminds me of a quote I read recently in “Media Worlds, Anthropology on New Terrain" by Faye D. Ginsburg, Lila Abu-Lughod, and Brian Larkin. The quote is by Raymond Willams and reads as follows, “It is often said that [media have] altered our world. In the same way, people often speak of a new world, a new society, a new phase of history, being created—“brought about”—by this or that new technology: the steam engine, the automobile, the atomic bomb. Most of us know what is generally implied when such things are said. But this may be the central difficulty: that we have got so used to statements of this general kind, in our most ordinary discussions, that we fail to realise their specific meanings. . . .

Yet all questions about cause and effect, as between technology and a society, are intensely practical. Until we have begun to answer them, we really do not know, in any particular sense, whether, for example, we are talking about a technology or about the uses of a technology; about necessary institutions or particular and changeable institutions; about a content or about a form. And this is not only a matter of intellectual uncertainty; it is a matter of social practice.” Murray and Williams both share a critique of technological determinism. In Williams quote, he argues that technologies do not create social change by themselves, they acquire meaning through human use. It feels easy to assert that “media changed the world”, but that oversimplifies our relationship with technology. Murray has a similar argument that narrative power of technology comes from the ways designers and users collaboratively shape interactive environments. Both authors treat technology as a cultural form rather than an autonomous force.

I also found the part about Eliza fascinating. The analogy Eliza : Weizenbaum :: ChatGPT : Sam Altman; highlights that both creators expressed serious concern about how their technologies and societies would interact. The technology alone could do no harm, but the narratives, institutions, expectations, and anxieties surrounding it could. Technologies and media forms don’t change society by themselves. Society determines how they are received, interpreted, and integrated. With the conclusion of Murray's chapter, I think artists have adapted to the essential properties of the digital environment. Users now have the agency to consume media in ways that have never been done. This notion of choice excites me.
